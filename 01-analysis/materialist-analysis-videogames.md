# Pixels and Power: A Materialist Analysis of Videogame History, Mechanics, and Ideology

---

## Preface: Why Materialism?

To analyze videogames materialistically is to refuse the comfortable fiction that games are "just entertainment." Every game is a product of specific labor relations, funded by specific capital, distributed through specific infrastructures, and consumed under specific conditions. Every mechanic encodes assumptions about how the world works — what counts as progress, who acts and who is acted upon, what constitutes winning and losing. These assumptions do not emerge from nowhere. They emerge from the material conditions under which games are produced and the ideological formations that those conditions sustain.

This analysis proceeds from three foundational premises drawn from the tradition of historical materialism:

1. **The base conditions the superstructure.** The economic organization of the game industry — who owns the means of production, who labors, who profits — shapes the cultural products that industry generates. A studio governed by quarterly earnings reports produces different objects than a cooperative of independent developers.

2. **Ideology operates through naturalization.** Following Gramsci, the most effective ideological work is that which renders contingent arrangements as inevitable, historical constructs as natural law. Game mechanics are exceptionally powerful vehicles for this operation because they do not merely *depict* systems — they *enact* them. The player does not watch capitalism; the player *performs* it.

3. **Contradictions are productive.** The game industry, like any capitalist enterprise, generates its own contradictions — between creative labor and profit extraction, between player agency and behavioral manipulation, between the utopian possibilities of interactive systems and their deployment as instruments of accumulation. These contradictions are not flaws in the analysis; they are its engine.

## Part I: Historical Material Analysis of the Industry

### 1.1 The Arcade Era: Coin-Operated Discipline (1971–1985)

The arcade cabinet is the ur-form of the videogame commodity, and its material logic is stark. The machine exists to extract quarters. Every design decision follows from this imperative. Difficulty curves are calibrated not to player satisfaction but to revenue per unit time — a game that can be mastered without additional payment is a failed product. The "game over" screen is not a ludic concept; it is a cash register.

This is not metaphor. Nolan Bushnell, founder of Atari, was explicit about the design philosophy: a game should be easy to learn (to attract the uninitiated) and impossible to master (to sustain revenue flow). The famous difficulty of early arcade games — *Pac-Man*, *Donkey Kong*, *Galaga* — is typically narrated as a charming feature of a bygone era. Materialist analysis reveals it as a disciplinary apparatus: the player's body is trained into a cycle of failure, payment, and retry. The quarter slot is the game's most important mechanic.

The arcade also established the spatial politics of gaming. Arcade cabinets were located in liminal commercial spaces — shopping malls, bowling alleys, laundromats, convenience stores. Access was mediated by geography, class, and parental supervision. The arcade was a site of unsupervised youth socialization, which provoked recurring moral panics — panics that were themselves class-coded, as middle-class anxieties about the corruption of children by commercial entertainment rarely extended to the consumption habits of the wealthy.

The labor conditions of the early industry were those of a nascent technology sector: informal, intensely exploitative, and mythologized as creative freedom. Early Atari was organized around what might be called *garage capitalism* — a flat organizational structure that masked the extraction of enormous surplus value from a small number of programmers. The 1979 departure of four Atari programmers to form Activision — the first third-party game developer — was a dispute over precisely this extraction. These programmers had generated hundreds of millions of dollars in revenue and received neither credit nor royalties. Their rebellion was not artistic but economic: a demand for a share of the surplus they produced.

### 1.2 The Console Wars: Platform as Capital (1985–2000)

Nintendo's resurrection of the North American console market after the 1983 crash established a model that persists today: the platform holder as gatekeeper. Nintendo's "Seal of Quality" program was not primarily about quality assurance. It was a mechanism of monopoly control. Third-party developers were required to purchase cartridges exclusively from Nintendo, were limited in the number of titles they could release per year, and were subject to content restrictions enforced at Nintendo's sole discretion. The platform was not a neutral infrastructure; it was a toll booth.

This model — in which the platform holder extracts rent from every transaction occurring on its infrastructure — is the direct ancestor of contemporary platform capitalism as theorized by Nick Srnicek. Apple's App Store, Valve's Steam, and Epic's Games Store all operate on the same fundamental logic: own the distribution channel, tax every exchange, and leverage that position to dictate terms to both producers and consumers.

The "console wars" between Nintendo and Sega, and later Sony, were narrated as a contest of innovation and consumer choice. They were in fact a contest over monopoly rents. Each company sought to establish a proprietary ecosystem — exclusive hardware, exclusive software, proprietary media formats — that would lock consumers into a single platform and lock developers into dependence on a single distribution channel. The consumer's "choice" of console was a choice of which monopolist to submit to.

The globalization of game production began in earnest during this period. Japanese companies dominated hardware manufacturing and much of software development, but this dominance was itself built on specific material conditions: Japan's postwar industrial policy, the suppression of organized labor, and the cultivation of a highly skilled, intensely disciplined technical workforce willing to accept working conditions — endemic overtime, rigid hierarchies, minimal individual credit — that would later be recognized as the template for "crunch culture."

### 1.3 The AAA Paradigm: Industrialization and Its Discontents (2000–2015)

The transition from cartridge to optical disc and then to digital distribution dramatically reduced the marginal cost of game reproduction while simultaneously inflating production budgets. This scissor dynamic — declining reproduction costs, rising production costs — drove rapid concentration of capital. Only firms with access to significant investment capital could fund the increasingly expensive productions that consumers expected. The result was an oligopolistic market structure dominated by a handful of major publishers: Electronic Arts, Activision Blizzard, Ubisoft, Take-Two Interactive, and their peers.

This concentration had predictable consequences for labor. The AAA studio became a factory in all but name. Hundreds of workers — programmers, artists, animators, sound designers, quality assurance testers, writers — were organized into hierarchical production pipelines, performing increasingly specialized tasks under managerial oversight. The romantic mythology of the lone genius designer persisted as an ideological cover for an industrial labor process.

"Crunch" — the industry's euphemism for systematic, unpaid or underpaid overtime — became the defining labor practice of this era. The 2004 "EA Spouse" blog post, in which the partner of an Electronic Arts developer publicly documented the human cost of mandatory 80-hour work weeks, was a landmark moment not because the practice was new but because it was finally named. Crunch is not an aberration or a failure of management; it is a structural feature of an industry that sells fixed-price commodities produced under conditions of high uncertainty. Because game sales are heavily front-loaded and subject to unpredictable consumer reception, publishers face strong incentives to minimize upfront labor costs — which means extracting maximum output from existing workers rather than hiring additional staff.

The global dimension of this exploitation deepened as well. Art asset production, quality assurance testing, localization, and customer support were increasingly outsourced to studios in Southeast Asia, Eastern Europe, and Latin America, where labor costs were lower and labor protections weaker. This spatial division of labor reproduced colonial patterns of extraction: creative and managerial functions remained concentrated in the imperial core (the United States, Western Europe, Japan), while routine and labor-intensive tasks were distributed to the periphery. The workers performing these tasks were largely invisible, their contributions uncredited and their labor conditions unexamined.

### 1.4 Live Service and Platform Capitalism: The Perpetual Extraction Machine (2015–Present)

The current era is defined by the convergence of several tendencies: the shift from product to service, the rise of microtransactions and loot boxes, the centrality of data extraction, and the intensification of behavioral manipulation.

The "live service" model — exemplified by *Fortnite*, *Genshin Impact*, *Destiny 2*, and *FIFA Ultimate Team* (now *EA Sports FC*) — represents a fundamental transformation in the commodity form of the game. The game is no longer a discrete product purchased once; it is an ongoing relationship, a subscription to a managed experience, a conduit through which value is continuously extracted from the player. The player is no longer merely a consumer but a resource — a source of revenue (through microtransactions), of data (through behavioral telemetry), and of content (through user-generated creations and social media engagement).

This model is sustained by sophisticated behavioral engineering derived from the same operant conditioning research that undergirds slot machine design. Variable-ratio reinforcement schedules, artificial scarcity, social pressure mechanics, loss aversion triggers, and "fear of missing out" (FOMO) systems are not incidental features but core design pillars. The loot box — a randomized virtual reward purchased with real currency — is the paradigmatic mechanic of this era. It is, in functional terms, a gambling mechanism deployed without the regulatory oversight applied to traditional gambling, targeted at a population that includes minors.

The concentration of capital has accelerated dramatically. Microsoft's acquisition of Activision Blizzard in 2023 for approximately $69 billion represented the largest acquisition in gaming history and signaled the industry's consolidation into a small number of platform conglomerates — Microsoft, Sony, Tencent, and to a lesser extent Nintendo and Apple — each seeking to control vertically integrated ecosystems encompassing hardware, software, distribution, and subscription services. Tencent's position is particularly significant: as the world's largest game company by revenue, with substantial ownership stakes in Riot Games, Epic Games, Supercell, and dozens of other studios, Tencent represents the interpenetration of Chinese state-adjacent capital and global game production in ways that complicate simple narratives of Western cultural imperialism.

## Part II: Mechanics as Ideology

### 2.1 Violence as Default Ontology

The overwhelming prevalence of violence as the primary interaction verb in mainstream games is not a question of creative laziness or technological limitation. It is an ideological structure. In the grammar of most games, violence is the transitive verb that connects subject (player) to object (world). The player *shoots*, *stabs*, *destroys*, *kills*. These are the verbs through which the player exercises agency. Non-violent verbs — *negotiate*, *care*, *grieve*, *maintain*, *repair* — are either absent or subordinated to the violent core.

This is a specific worldview with specific material origins. The U.S. military's long-standing relationship with the game industry — from the Army's funding of early computer research to the direct collaboration that produced *America's Army* (2002) to the broader ideological alignment between military doctrine and game design — has produced what scholars such as Nick Dyer-Witheford and Greig de Peuter call "games of empire." These are games in which the player enacts the logic of military intervention: identify threat, mobilize force, neutralize opposition, secure territory.

Consider *Call of Duty: Modern Warfare 2* (2009), specifically the "No Russian" mission, in which the player participates in a mass shooting of civilians at an airport. The mission was controversial, but the controversy focused on the content — the depiction of civilian murder — rather than the structure. The structural point is that the game's entire ludic grammar is built around pulling a trigger at a target. The "No Russian" mission did not introduce violence into a peaceful game; it revealed, through transgression, the violence that was always already the game's fundamental mode of being.

The *Dark Souls* series (2011–2022) offers a more subtle case. Often celebrated for its difficulty and "fair" design, the Souls games nonetheless operate within a combat-centric ontology. The world is populated almost exclusively by entities whose function is to be killed. Non-player characters exist primarily to provide resources (items, information, upgrades) that facilitate more killing. The "difficulty" that players celebrate is the difficulty of mastering the rhythm and timing of combat — that is, the difficulty of becoming a more efficient instrument of violence. The game is widely praised for respecting the player's intelligence and agency; what it respects, specifically, is the player's capacity for lethal optimization.

### 2.2 Optimization, Efficiency, and the Accumulation Loop

The core loop of most games can be described in terms directly borrowed from the circuit of capital: invest resources (time, currency, materials) → engage in production (combat, crafting, questing) → extract surplus (loot, experience points, currency) → reinvest surplus in expanded production capacity (better equipment, higher levels, new abilities) → repeat at a higher level of intensity.

This is not M-C-M′ by analogy. It *is* M-C-M′, transposed into a ludic register. The player's relationship to the game economy mirrors the capitalist's relationship to the real economy: the accumulation of value is not a means to an end but the end itself. The RPG level-up system, the loot treadmill, the crafting economy — these are not neutral design patterns. They are simulations of accumulation logic, and they train the player to experience accumulation as pleasurable, as the natural and desirable trajectory of activity.

*Diablo* (1996) and its descendants are exemplary. The game's feedback loop — kill monsters, acquire randomly generated loot, equip better loot, kill harder monsters, acquire better loot — is a pure expression of accumulation drive. The pleasure of the game is not narrative or aesthetic; it is the pleasure of watching numbers go up, of seeing the inventory fill, of feeling the power curve steepen. This is the pleasure of capital accumulation, abstracted and aestheticized.

The "idle game" genre — *Cookie Clicker* (2013), *Adventure Capitalist* (2015), *Clicker Heroes* (2014) — strips this logic to its bare essence. These games automate the accumulation loop entirely; the player's role is reduced to periodic optimization of the accumulation rate. The genre's name is revealing: the ideal subject of capital is the idle capitalist, the rentier, the owner who accumulates without laboring. That these games are understood as "parodies" of capitalism while simultaneously reproducing its core logic is itself ideologically significant — the parodic frame serves as a prophylactic against critical engagement with the structure being parodied.

### 2.3 Expansion, Conquest, and Extraction Systems

The strategy genre, particularly the 4X subgenre (eXplore, eXpand, eXploit, eXterminate), organizes its mechanics around the logic of imperial expansion. This will be examined in detail in Part III. Here it is sufficient to note that the 4X framework — first articulated by Alan Emrich in a 1993 review of *Master of Orion* — is not a neutral description of gameplay possibilities. It is a condensation of imperial logic into four verbs, each of which presupposes a specific relationship between the player-subject and the world-object.

*eXplore* presupposes that the world is unknown and awaiting discovery by the player — a colonial epistemology that denies the prior knowledge and habitation of the spaces being "explored." *eXpand* presupposes that territorial growth is both possible and desirable — that the player's domain should grow, that stasis is stagnation. *eXploit* presupposes that the world's value lies in its utility to the player — that forests are lumber, mountains are minerals, rivers are trade routes. *eXterminate* presupposes that competing claims to these resources are to be resolved through elimination rather than coexistence.

### 2.4 Scarcity as Design Philosophy

Most game economies are built on artificial scarcity. Resources are limited, inventory space is constrained, currencies must be earned or purchased. This scarcity is a design choice, not a necessity. Digital goods have zero marginal cost of reproduction; there is no material reason why a virtual sword should be scarce. The scarcity is imposed to create the conditions under which accumulation becomes meaningful, competition becomes necessary, and monetization becomes possible.

This design philosophy naturalizes a particular economic worldview: that scarcity is the fundamental condition of existence, that competition for scarce resources is the natural mode of social organization, and that the efficient allocation of scarce resources is the central problem of political life. This is, not coincidentally, the foundational assumption of neoclassical economics. Game design does not merely reflect this assumption; it trains players to inhabit it as self-evident reality.

The contrast with games that experiment with abundance is instructive. *Minecraft* in Creative Mode provides unlimited resources and eliminates survival pressures, producing a radically different player experience — one centered on creation rather than extraction, on expression rather than accumulation. That Creative Mode is widely regarded as the "lesser" mode, the mode for children or casual players, reveals how deeply the ideology of scarcity has been internalized: a game without scarcity is not a "real" game.

### 2.5 The Individual Hero and the Erasure of Collective Action

The default subject-position offered by mainstream games is that of the individual hero — a singular agent of exceptional ability whose actions determine the fate of the world. This is the liberal subject in ludic form: autonomous, self-determining, unencumbered by social relations. The hero's power derives not from collective organization but from personal attributes — skill, equipment, level, innate talent.

This individualism is so pervasive as to be nearly invisible. Even in games that feature party-based mechanics — *Final Fantasy*, *Mass Effect*, *Dragon Age* — the player controls a single protagonist who makes all significant decisions. Party members are resources to be managed, not agents with independent political commitments or conflicting material interests. When party members do express disagreement, it is typically resolvable through persuasion or charisma checks — a liberal fantasy of consensus-through-rhetoric that effaces the reality of irreconcilable material conflicts.

Multiplayer games might seem to offer a counterexample, but the dominant modes of multiplayer — competitive PvP and cooperative PvE — reproduce individualist and market logic in different registers. Competitive multiplayer is a zero-sum contest in which one player's gain is another's loss, mediated by individual skill. Cooperative multiplayer is a division of labor in which specialized roles (tank, healer, DPS) combine to produce outcomes none could achieve alone — but this is the cooperation of the production line, not the solidarity of collective action. The team cooperates to *defeat* an external enemy, not to *transform* a shared condition.

## Part III: Colonialism and Empire in Game Systems

### 3.1 Terra Nullius: Land as Empty Resource

The strategy game begins with a map. Typically, this map is shrouded in "fog of war" — the space is unknown, waiting to be revealed by the player's exploration. This is the cartographic fantasy of the colonial encounter: the land exists only when the colonizer sees it. Before the player's scouts arrive, the territory is, in functional terms, nothing. It has no history, no inhabitants with prior claims, no political complexity. It is raw material awaiting transformation.

*Civilization* (1991–present) is the paradigmatic case. The game begins with a single settler unit placed on an empty (or nearly empty) map. The player founds a city, which then projects influence over surrounding tiles. These tiles are valued exclusively for the resources they yield: food, production, gold, science, culture. A forest is not an ecosystem; it is a production bonus. A mountain is not a geological formation; it is a science modifier. The land is legible only through the grammar of extraction.

The game does include "barbarian" encampments — hostile non-player entities that occupy territory and attack the player's units. These barbarians are not civilizations; they have no cities, no culture, no technology, no diplomacy. They are obstacles to be cleared, nuisances to be eliminated. Their function in the game system is precisely the function assigned to indigenous peoples in colonial ideology: they occupy space that the civilized player could put to productive use, and their removal is a necessary step in the march of progress.

When *Civilization* does represent indigenous peoples, it does so through the same system that represents European colonial powers: as "civilizations" with unique bonuses, unit types, and building options. The Aztecs, the Zulu, the Iroquois, and the Maori are rendered commensurable with England, Germany, and America — flattened into interchangeable player-states competing on equal terms within a shared mechanical framework. This formal equality erases the material inequality of the actual colonial encounter. In *Civilization*, there is no colonialism because the game's structure cannot represent asymmetric power relations between civilizations — only symmetric competition.

### 3.2 The Technology Tree as Teleological Narrative

The technology tree — a branching structure of sequential discoveries that unlock new capabilities — is one of the most pervasive mechanics in strategy games, and one of the most ideologically loaded. It encodes a specific philosophy of history: that human development follows a linear, progressive, universal trajectory from "primitive" to "advanced," and that this trajectory can be accelerated through rational investment in research.

This is Whig history as game mechanic. The technology tree in *Civilization* runs from "Agriculture" and "Animal Husbandry" through "Gunpowder" and "Industrialization" to "Nuclear Fission" and "Future Tech." Every civilization follows the same path, differs only in speed, and arrives at the same destination. There is no possibility of alternative modernities, of development paths that do not converge on Western industrial capitalism, of valid social organizations that are not "stages" on the way to something else.

The ideological function of the tech tree is to naturalize Western technological development as the only path and to render all other social formations as failures to progress — as societies "stuck" at earlier nodes of the tree. This is precisely the logic that justified colonial intervention: the colonized were "behind," and colonialism was the (benevolent or brutal) mechanism by which they would be brought "forward."

*Age of Empires II* (1999) illustrates a subtler version of this logic. The game organizes history into four "ages" — Dark, Feudal, Castle, and Imperial — through which every civilization must advance. The mechanical requirements for advancing are consistent: accumulate resources, construct specific buildings, pay a fee. The message is clear: historical development is a resource management problem, and all civilizations face fundamentally the same problem. The material specificity of historical change — the role of class struggle, ecological transformation, contingent events, and uneven development — is erased in favor of a universal narrative of rational accumulation.

### 3.3 Resource Extraction as Gameplay Core

In *Age of Empires*, the primary activity is resource gathering. Villagers chop wood, mine gold, harvest food, and quarry stone. These resources are converted into military units, buildings, and technological upgrades. The game's core loop is: extract resources → build military → conquer territory → extract more resources.

This loop is the logic of colonial extraction rendered as gameplay. The land exists to be harvested. The appropriate relationship to the environment is one of instrumental exploitation. There is no mechanic for sustainability, for ecological limits, for the consequences of deforestation or mineral depletion (resources are, in most RTS games, finite but regenerate or are functionally inexhaustible on the relevant timescale). The player who extracts most efficiently wins.

The workers who perform this extraction are themselves resources. In *Age of Empires*, villagers are produced from the Town Center for a fixed cost and are functionally interchangeable. They have no needs beyond the food that sustains the overall population count, no desires, no capacity for resistance. They are, in the language of classical political economy, variable capital — labor-power purchased for a price and deployed to generate surplus value. That the game refers to them as "villagers" rather than "workers" is itself ideological: it invokes a pastoral, pre-industrial image that obscures the extractive logic of their function.

*Stellaris* (2016), a 4X space strategy game, makes the colonial logic even more explicit by allowing the player to enslave conquered alien populations, purge them from occupied planets, or subject them to "forced labor" — terms the game uses without irony. The game includes mechanics for managing "faction happiness" and "unrest" among subjugated populations, essentially gamifying the colonial administration of occupied territories. That these mechanics exist alongside the option to play as a benevolent democratic federation does not neutralize their ideological content; it reproduces the liberal fantasy that imperialism is a choice rather than a structural tendency of the systems the game models.

### 3.4 Winning as Domination

In most strategy games, victory conditions are structured around domination. *Civilization* offers multiple paths to victory — military conquest, scientific achievement, cultural influence, diplomatic consensus — but all of them require the player to outperform all other civilizations on a single metric. Even the "diplomatic victory" requires accumulating enough political capital to effectively dominate the world congress. The game cannot model a world of genuine political pluralism because its victory conditions require a single winner.

This is the competitive logic of capital transposed onto the plane of civilizational history. The world is a zero-sum arena in which civilizations compete for finite resources, and the goal is to win — to emerge as the sole dominant power. Coexistence is not a victory condition. Mutual flourishing is not a victory condition. The only stable state is hegemony.

The *Total War* series (2000–present) dispenses with even the pretense of non-military victory conditions. The series title is descriptive: the game is about total war, and the objective is the total subjugation of all competing factions. Diplomacy exists but is subordinated to military strategy — alliances are temporary expedients, trade agreements are means of accumulation, and every diplomatic relationship is evaluated in terms of its contribution to eventual military dominance.

## Part IV: Capitalism Simulation and Internalization

### 4.1 Tycoon Games: The Entrepreneur as Player

The "tycoon" genre — *RollerCoaster Tycoon* (1999), *Transport Tycoon* (1994), *Game Dev Tycoon* (2012), *Planet Coaster* (2016) — places the player in the role of a capitalist entrepreneur. The player's objective is to build a profitable business by managing resources, optimizing operations, and satisfying consumer demand. The pleasure of the game is the pleasure of accumulation and control: watching revenue graphs climb, expanding operations, unlocking new capabilities through reinvested profits.

These games are ideologically potent because they position the player as capital rather than labor. The player never experiences the perspective of the roller coaster operator, the park custodian, the game developer grinding through crunch. These figures exist in the game only as costs — line items in the budget, headcounts to be optimized, wages to be minimized. The worker's perspective is structurally invisible because the game's subject-position is that of the owner.

*Game Dev Tycoon* is a particularly instructive case because it simulates the very industry that produced it. The player manages a game development studio, making decisions about genre, platform, team size, and development focus. The game rewards the player for producing profitable titles and penalizes them for overspending on development. At no point does the game model crunch, worker burnout, gender discrimination, or any of the labor conditions that define actual game development. The game industry, as modeled by *Game Dev Tycoon*, is a frictionless market in which success is determined by smart investment and creative vision — the entrepreneurial fantasy that the industry sells about itself.

### 4.2 Management Sims: Optimization as Ethics

*Two Point Hospital* (2018), *Prison Architect* (2015), and *Cities: Skylines* (2015) each place the player in a managerial role. The player optimizes systems — hospital workflows, prison layouts, urban infrastructure — to achieve efficiency metrics. The games are polished, charming, and deeply ideological.

*Prison Architect* is especially revealing. The player designs and manages a private prison, balancing construction costs, staffing needs, security concerns, and inmate welfare. The game includes mechanics for solitary confinement, parole hearings, contraband searches, and prisoner labor programs. The player can profit from inmate labor, investing the proceeds in expanded facilities that house more inmates who generate more labor revenue. This is the prison-industrial complex rendered as a management puzzle — and the puzzle is enjoyable, which is precisely the point.

The game does include a "Reform" system that allows the player to offer educational and therapeutic programs to inmates, reducing recidivism. But this reform system exists within the same optimization framework as everything else: it is a cost-benefit calculation, a tool for reducing the negative externalities (riots, escapes) that threaten the player's bottom line. The game cannot model abolition because the game *is* the prison. To stop building prisons is to stop playing.

### 4.3 Idle Games: The Abstraction of Extraction

The idle game represents the terminal point of capitalist game design. In *Cookie Clicker*, the player clicks a cookie to generate cookies. Cookies are invested in upgrades that generate cookies automatically. The game plays itself, accumulating cookies at an exponential rate while the player watches or does other things. The player's role is reduced to periodic interventions that optimize the rate of accumulation.

This is not a parody of capitalism; it is its apotheosis. The idle game models the ideal of financial capital: money that makes money without labor, value that self-valorizes, accumulation that requires no effort beyond the initial investment. That players find this satisfying — that there is genuine pleasure in watching numbers grow without action — reveals how thoroughly the logic of accumulation has been internalized as a source of psychological reward.

*Adventure Capitalist* makes the ideology explicit in its title and presentation. The player manages a portfolio of businesses, reinvesting profits to accelerate growth. The game's visual design is cheerful and cartoonish, its tone ironic and self-aware. But ironic distance does not neutralize ideological function; if anything, it reinforces it by inoculating the player against the discomfort of recognizing what they are enjoying. The player can simultaneously acknowledge that the game is "about" capitalist exploitation and continue to play it with pleasure — a textbook example of what Slavoj Žižek calls "cynical ideology," in which the subject knows very well what they are doing but does it anyway.

### 4.4 Free-to-Play and the Monetization of Desire

The free-to-play model completes the circuit between simulated capitalism and actual capitalism. In games like *Clash of Clans* (2012), *Candy Crush Saga* (2012), and *Genshin Impact* (2020), the player is simultaneously performing capitalist logic within the game (accumulating resources, optimizing systems, competing for dominance) and being subjected to capitalist logic by the game (through microtransactions, artificial wait times, and gacha mechanics).

The "gacha" system — named after Japanese capsule toy machines — is particularly significant. The player pays real money for a random chance at a desirable virtual item, with probability distributions heavily weighted toward undesirable outcomes. This is a commodified lottery, a formalized system of manufactured desire and managed disappointment. The player knows the odds are against them but pays anyway because the game has been engineered to produce intense desire for the rare item — through social comparison (other players displaying their rare pulls), narrative investment (characters with elaborate backstories and emotional appeal), and mechanical advantage (rare characters are measurably more powerful).

*Genshin Impact* generates billions of dollars annually through this system, predominantly from a small percentage of high-spending players (derisively called "whales" in industry parlance — the cetacean metaphor is telling, recalling Melville's Ahab-like pursuit of the great prize). The term "whale" itself encodes the predatory relationship: the player is not a customer but prey, a large catch, a source of extraction. The rest of the player base — the "free" players — serve as content, as the social environment that makes the whale's spending meaningful. The free player's labor (time spent playing, social media engagement, community participation) produces the context of value within which the whale's purchases have meaning.

## Part V: Hegemony and Player Subjectivity

### 5.1 Gramsci and the Controller

Antonio Gramsci's concept of cultural hegemony describes the process by which the ruling class maintains its dominance not (only) through coercion but through the production of consent — by making its particular worldview appear as universal common sense. The dominant class does not merely impose its will; it shapes the conceptual framework through which the dominated understand their own condition, rendering exploitation invisible by making it seem natural, inevitable, or even desirable.

Games are exceptionally powerful instruments of hegemonic production because they do not merely represent ideological positions — they *proceduralize* them. Ian Bogost's concept of "procedural rhetoric" captures part of this: games make arguments through their mechanics, through the systems they model and the behaviors they reward. But the concept does not go far enough. Procedural rhetoric implies a deliberate communicative act, a designer with a message. Hegemony operates precisely where there is no deliberate message — where the designer has simply reproduced the assumptions of their own social position as the neutral, default structure of the game world.

When a designer creates a game in which progress is measured by accumulation, in which problems are solved through violence, in which the player-subject is an individual agent competing against others for scarce resources — the designer is not, in most cases, making a conscious argument for capitalism, militarism, or individualism. They are simply building a game that "makes sense," that follows the conventions of the genre, that feels "right." This unselfconscious reproduction of ideology is precisely what makes it hegemonic. The most effective ideology is the one that does not need to be argued because it has already been accepted as the framework within which arguments take place.

### 5.2 The Illusion of Choice

Contemporary game design places enormous emphasis on "player choice" — branching narratives, moral decision systems, open worlds, multiple endings. This rhetoric of choice serves an important ideological function: it positions the player as a free agent operating within a responsive system, exercising meaningful autonomy.

But the choices offered are always choices within a predetermined structure. The player of *Mass Effect* can choose to be a "Paragon" or a "Renegade," but they cannot choose to be a pacifist, a deserter, or a revolutionary. The player of *Skyrim* can choose which faction to join, but they cannot choose to organize the peasantry against the aristocracy. The player of *Cyberpunk 2077* can choose how to navigate the corporate dystopia, but they cannot choose to overthrow it. The game presents itself as an open field of possibility while constraining the player within a specific ideological corridor.

This is liberalism as game design. The liberal subject is "free to choose" — free to select from a menu of options curated by power. The menu itself is not open to negotiation. In political life, this manifests as the choice between competing candidates who agree on fundamental economic premises. In game design, it manifests as the choice between narrative paths that converge on the same structural logic: individual agency, competitive advancement, accumulation of power.

The *Fallout* series illustrates this with particular clarity. The games present elaborate moral dilemmas — should the player side with this faction or that one, should they save this settlement or sacrifice it — but the underlying mechanical structure rewards the same behaviors regardless of moral alignment: kill enemies, loot their possessions, complete quests for experience and rewards, become more powerful. The player's "moral choices" are superstructural variations on a mechanical base that never changes. The game's ideology is not in its narrative but in its systems.

### 5.3 The Naturalization of Systems

Perhaps the most significant hegemonic operation performed by games is the naturalization of systems — the process by which players come to experience designed, contingent rule-sets as objective, immutable structures.

When a player says "the economy is broken" about a game, they mean that the game's economic system is not functioning as intended — that prices are too high, rewards too low, drop rates too stingy. They do not mean that the concept of having a price-based economy in the game is itself a design choice that could have been made differently. The economy is taken as given; only its parameters are up for debate.

This mirrors the ideological operation that Mark Fisher described as "capitalist realism" — the pervasive sense that capitalism is the only viable economic system, that alternatives are either impossible or undesirable. In Fisher's formulation, "it is easier to imagine the end of the world than the end of capitalism." In game design terms, it is easier to imagine rebalancing the economy than imagining a game without one. The systemic structure has been naturalized to the point where it is no longer perceived as a structure at all.

Players who spend hundreds of hours within a game system develop an intimate, embodied knowledge of its rules. They learn what behaviors are rewarded and what behaviors are punished. They internalize the system's values as their own values — not through persuasion but through practice. The player who has spent months optimizing their resource extraction in *Civilization* has not been *told* that efficient extraction is good; they have *experienced* it as good, felt the satisfaction of a well-managed economy, the frustration of waste and inefficiency. This experiential pedagogy is far more effective than any explicit argument because it operates below the level of conscious critical engagement.

## Part VI: Counterexamples and Resistance

### 6.1 Papers, Please (2013): Bureaucracy and Moral Complicity

*Papers, Please*, designed by Lucas Pope, places the player in the role of an immigration inspector at the border checkpoint of a fictional Soviet-bloc country. The player's task is to review the documents of people seeking entry, verifying that their papers are in order and denying entry to those with discrepancies.

The game's critical power lies in its mechanical construction of complicity. The player is paid per person processed. Their family's survival — the ability to pay for food, heat, and medicine — depends on processing as many applicants as possible. Thorough inspection takes time; compassion costs money. When a refugee presents forged documents, the player faces a genuine dilemma: follow the rules (and maintain their income) or break them (and risk their family's survival). The game makes the player feel, in their body, the material pressures that compel obedience to unjust systems.

This is a materialist game not because it depicts material conditions (though it does) but because its mechanics *enact* them. The player does not observe the relationship between economic precarity and complicity with state violence; they live it. The game's ideological intervention is not in its narrative or aesthetics but in its systems — in the way the pay-per-inspection mechanic transforms moral choice from an abstract exercise into a material calculation.

### 6.2 Disco Elysium (2019): Ideology as Explicit System

*Disco Elysium*, developed by ZA/UM, is remarkable for the degree to which it treats political ideology as a subject of explicit, sustained, and sophisticated engagement. The game's character system is organized around a set of "skills" that represent different cognitive and emotional capacities — logic, empathy, authority, espionage — and these skills actively comment on the game's events, offering competing interpretations drawn from different ideological traditions.

The game includes an explicit political alignment system with four quadrants — communist, fascist, ultraliberal, and moralist — each of which is treated with a specific combination of sympathy and critique. Crucially, the game does not present these positions as equivalent "choices" to be selected from a menu. Each position is embedded in a specific material context — the post-revolutionary history of the fictional city of Revachol, the economic devastation wrought by foreign intervention, the class composition of the player's precinct — and each is shown to have specific consequences for specific people.

The game's most radical formal innovation is its treatment of the protagonist's interiority. The player character is an amnesiac alcoholic — a man whose identity has been destroyed, who must reconstruct himself from fragments. This narrative device serves a critical function: it de-naturalizes the player-subject. The player cannot take the protagonist's identity for granted; they must actively construct it, choosing which voices to listen to, which memories to pursue, which ideological commitments to make. The game reveals subjectivity itself as a political construction, not a natural given.

*Disco Elysium* also refuses the violence-as-default-verb structure of most RPGs. The game includes combat, but it is rare, clumsy, and consequential. The primary mode of interaction is conversation — extended, deeply written, ideologically charged conversation. The game's mechanics reward the player not for killing enemies but for thinking carefully, listening attentively, and engaging with complex ideas. This is a genuine alternative to the dominant ludic grammar, and it demonstrates that games can be mechanically engaging without centering violence.

### 6.3 This War of Mine (2014): War from Below

*This War of Mine*, developed by 11 bit studios, reverses the typical perspective of the war game. Instead of controlling soldiers on the battlefield, the player manages a group of civilians trying to survive in a besieged city. The game's mechanics center on scarcity, desperation, and moral compromise: scavenging for food and medicine, maintaining shelter, managing the physical and psychological health of the group, and making impossible choices — steal from the elderly couple, or let your own people starve?

The game's critical intervention is perspectival. By shifting the camera from the combatant to the civilian, the game reveals what conventional war games conceal: that war is not a contest between armies but a catastrophe inflicted upon populations. The mechanics reinforce this: there is no "winning" in *This War of Mine*, only surviving. The game ends when the ceasefire is declared or when all the characters are dead. The player does not achieve victory; they endure.

The game also models the psychological consequences of violence in ways that most games do not. Characters who are forced to steal, to kill, or to witness suffering develop depression and trauma. These are not cosmetic effects; they are mechanical states that reduce the character's functionality and can lead to suicide. The game creates a genuine mechanical disincentive for violence — not through moralizing narration but through systemic feedback that makes the player feel the cost of their choices.

### 6.4 Indie and Experimental Works: Expanding the Possible

Beyond these well-known examples, a broader ecology of independent and experimental games explores alternatives to dominant design paradigms:

- *Celeste* (2018) reframes the platformer's challenge not as conquest but as the protagonist's struggle with mental illness. The mountain is not an enemy to be defeated but a metaphor for the internal work of self-acceptance. The assist mode — which allows players to adjust difficulty without penalty — embodies a design philosophy of accessibility as care.

- *Stardew Valley* (2016) offers a pastoral counterpoint to the extraction-and-expansion logic of mainstream games. The player farms, builds relationships, and participates in a small community. While the game does include accumulation mechanics (farming is, after all, a form of production), its rhythms are cyclical rather than exponential, its goals social rather than competitive. The game begins with the player's character quitting their job at a soul-crushing corporation — an explicit, if gentle, anti-capitalist gesture.

- *Unpacking* (2021) constructs an entire narrative through the act of arranging objects in domestic spaces. There are no enemies, no resources, no competition. The game's "mechanic" is care — the careful placement of someone's belongings, the attention to what a coffee mug or a stuffed animal reveals about a life.

- *A Short Hike* (2019) creates a small open world whose pleasure lies not in conquest or extraction but in movement, exploration, and casual encounter. The game has no fail state, no enemies, no competition. It is an exercise in being present in a space, and its design demonstrates that games can generate engagement without generating anxiety.

These games do not, individually or collectively, constitute a revolutionary practice. They exist within the same market structures, the same platform ecologies, the same patterns of consumption as the games they critique. But they demonstrate that the dominant design paradigms are not inevitable — that the current alignment of mechanics and ideology is a historical product, not a natural law, and that other alignments are possible.

## Part VII: Design Implications — Toward Non-Hegemonic Mechanics

### 7.1 The Problem of "Alternative" Design

To propose "non-hegemonic" game mechanics is to confront a fundamental difficulty: the designer is never outside the ideological field they seek to critique. The tools of game design — reward loops, feedback systems, optimization structures — are themselves products of the same instrumental rationality that characterizes capitalist production. To "use the master's tools to dismantle the master's house" is a familiar problem in critical theory, and it applies with particular force to game design, where the "tools" are not merely instruments but structures of thought.

Any proposal for alternative mechanics must therefore proceed with epistemic humility, recognizing that the attempt to design "outside" ideology is itself an ideological gesture. What follows is not a blueprint but a set of provocations — design directions that take seriously the possibility of games organized around principles other than accumulation, competition, and domination.

### 7.2 Cooperation Without Domination

Most cooperative games substitute a shared enemy for a competing player: the group cooperates to defeat an external threat. This is not an alternative to competitive logic but a displacement of it — the competition is still present, it has merely been redirected outward. Genuine cooperative design would need to model cooperation not as a means to victory but as a practice with its own intrinsic rewards.

This might involve mechanics of mutual aid — systems in which the player's well-being is mechanically linked to the well-being of others, such that helping another player is not altruistic (sacrificing one's own interest for another's) but constitutive (one's own flourishing depends on the flourishing of the collective). *Spiritfarer* (2020) approaches this design: the player cares for spirits preparing for death, and the game's primary loop is the maintenance of relationships rather than the accumulation of power. But even *Spiritfarer* ultimately resolves into a management sim — the player manages the spirits' needs, not the spirits themselves.

A more radical design might eliminate the singular player-subject altogether, creating games in which the "player" is a collective whose composition and decision-making processes are themselves the subject of play. What would a game look like if the fundamental unit of agency were not the individual but the assembly?

### 7.3 Care Instead of Extraction

To design around care rather than extraction is to reorient the game's core loop from taking to maintaining. In an extraction-based game, the player's relationship to the world is subtractive — the world has resources, the player takes them, and the world is diminished. In a care-based game, the player's relationship to the world would be sustaining — the player's actions maintain, repair, and enrich the world, and the game's challenge lies in the complexity and fragility of the systems being maintained.

This is not purely speculative. Gardening games, pet care simulations, and games like *Katamari Damacy* (2004) — which operates through gathering rather than destroying — gesture toward this possibility. But a fully realized care-based design would need to avoid the trap of "care as management" — the reduction of care to an optimization problem, a set of needs to be efficiently satisfied. Genuine care is relational, unpredictable, and emotionally demanding. Modeling this mechanically is a profound design challenge, but it is a challenge worth undertaking.

### 7.4 Coexistence Instead of Conquest

The default spatial logic of games is territorial — the player expands their domain, pushing boundaries outward, claiming more space. An alternative spatial logic might center on borders as sites of encounter rather than conflict — on the negotiation of shared spaces, the management of difference, the construction of commons.

A game might model an ecosystem in which multiple species (or factions, or communities) occupy overlapping territories, and the challenge lies not in conquering territory but in negotiating coexistence — managing the tensions and mutualities that arise when different forms of life share space. This would require mechanics of compromise, of partial satisfaction, of outcomes in which no party gets everything it wants but the whole persists.

### 7.5 Cyclical Time vs. Progressive Time

Most games model time as linear and progressive — the player moves "forward" through levels, chapters, or technological eras, and going back is either impossible or undesirable. This progressive temporality encodes a specific view of history: that time moves in one direction, that change is cumulative, and that the present is always an improvement on the past.

An alternative might model cyclical time — seasons, generations, ecological cycles — in which the challenge lies not in advancing to the next stage but in maintaining equilibrium across recurring patterns. Some farming games approach this (the seasonal rhythms of *Stardew Valley*, for instance), but a fully developed cyclical design would need to make the cycle itself meaningful — to create stakes around the question of whether the cycle continues, and to model the consequences of breaking it.

### 7.6 Opacity and Ambiguity vs. Transparency and Optimization

Most game systems are designed to be legible — the player can see the numbers, understand the mechanics, and optimize their behavior accordingly. This legibility is a design value imported from engineering and management science: the system should be transparent so that it can be controlled.

An alternative design philosophy might embrace opacity — systems whose workings are partially hidden, whose outcomes are uncertain, whose logic cannot be fully grasped by the individual player. This would create a different kind of engagement, one based not on mastery and control but on attention, interpretation, and humility. *Rain World* (2017) experiments with this approach: its ecosystem is complex, dynamic, and largely indifferent to the player, who must learn to navigate it through observation rather than domination.

## Part VIII: Self-Critique, Expansion, and Analytical Framework

### 8.1 Critical Evaluation of Blind Spots

This analysis has several significant limitations that must be acknowledged:

**Geographic and linguistic bias.** The analysis is overwhelmingly focused on games produced in the United States, Western Europe, and Japan, and on games available in English. This reproduces the very center-periphery dynamics the analysis critiques. The game industries of China, South Korea, Brazil, Nigeria, Indonesia, and other regions produce enormous quantities of games under specific material conditions and with specific ideological content that this analysis does not examine. Chinese mobile games, for instance, operate within a regulatory environment shaped by the Chinese Communist Party's content policies, and their ideological content cannot be adequately understood through a framework derived entirely from Western critical theory.

**Player agency and reception.** This analysis treats players primarily as subjects constituted by game systems — as absorbers of ideology rather than active interpreters and resistors. This is a common limitation of ideological critique: it privileges the text over the reader, the system over the user. In practice, players are far more active, creative, and resistant than this framework acknowledges. Modding communities, speedrunning cultures, fan production, and player-organized protests (such as the *World of Warcraft* Corrupted Blood incident being studied by epidemiologists, or the *EVE Online* worker strikes) demonstrate that players are not passive recipients of designed ideologies but active agents who appropriate, subvert, and transform game systems.

**Gender and sexuality.** This analysis addresses gender and sexuality only obliquely, despite their centrality to both the material conditions of game production (the industry's well-documented problems with sexism, harassment, and discrimination) and the ideological content of game systems (the construction of masculinity through violence, the sexualization of female characters, the marginalization of queer identities). A more complete analysis would need to integrate feminist and queer theory as constitutive elements of the framework, not supplementary considerations.

**Race.** Similarly, this analysis does not adequately address the racial dimensions of game ideology — the ways in which game systems construct racial categories, reproduce racial hierarchies, and normalize racial violence. The colonial analysis in Part III gestures toward this, but a full treatment would need to engage with critical race theory, Afro-pessimism, and the specific histories of racial representation in games.

**Disability.** The analysis does not address disability, accessibility, or the ways in which game design constructs particular bodies as normative and others as deficient. This is a significant omission given the material consequences of inaccessible design and the growing discourse around disability justice in game spaces.

**Ecological crisis.** While the analysis discusses extraction as an ideological structure in games, it does not adequately address the material ecological footprint of the game industry itself — the energy consumption of data centers, the mineral extraction required for hardware production, the electronic waste generated by planned obsolescence — or the ways in which games model (or fail to model) ecological systems.

### 8.2 Expanding the Weakest Sections

The weakest section of this analysis is the discussion of counterexamples and resistance (Part VI), which risks two complementary errors: first, overstating the radical potential of individual games that remain embedded in capitalist production and distribution systems; and second, understating the cumulative significance of a broad ecology of alternative design practices.

A stronger account would situate these games within the broader landscape of independent game production, examining the material conditions that enable and constrain alternative design. The tools of independent production — engines like Unity and Godot, distribution platforms like itch.io, funding mechanisms like Kickstarter and Patreon — are themselves products of specific economic arrangements. Unity is a for-profit corporation; itch.io operates within the same platform logic as Steam, albeit with a more permissive revenue-sharing model; Kickstarter monetizes creative risk-taking. The "independence" of independent games is always relative, always conditioned by the infrastructure on which it depends.

A stronger account would also attend to the collective, organizational dimensions of alternative game production — cooperatives like Motion Twin (developers of *Dead Cells*), worker-owned studios, unionization efforts like those of Game Workers Unite, and the growing discourse around game design as a form of political organizing. These organizational experiments are as important as the games they produce, because they demonstrate that alternative relations of production are possible within the industry, not only alternative products.

Furthermore, the analysis should acknowledge traditions of game-making outside the commercial sphere entirely: educational games designed for social justice, therapeutic games, community-designed tabletop RPGs, and indigenous game design practices that draw on traditions older than digital technology. These practices challenge not only the ideological content of mainstream games but the very framework of "game" as commodity.

### 8.3 A Framework for Materialist Game Analysis

Drawing from this analysis, the following framework can be applied to any game:

#### I. Material Conditions of Production

- **Who made this game?** Under what labor conditions? In what country/countries? With what division of labor between creative, technical, and support roles?
- **Who funded it?** What capital structures enabled its production? What return on investment was expected, and how does that expectation shape the product?
- **Who distributes it?** Through what platforms? Under what revenue-sharing arrangements? With what gatekeeper controls?
- **Who profits?** How is revenue distributed among owners, investors, managers, and workers?

#### II. Material Conditions of Consumption

- **Who plays this game?** What is the class, gender, racial, and geographic composition of the player base? What material conditions (hardware, internet access, leisure time) are required for play?
- **What does playing cost?** Purchase price, subscription fees, microtransaction spending, time investment. Who is paying, and who is being paid?
- **What data does the game extract?** Behavioral telemetry, social connections, purchasing patterns. How is this data used, and by whom?

#### III. Systemic Ideology

- **What is the core loop?** What activities does the game reward, and what activities does it punish or render impossible?
- **What is the game's ontology?** What entities exist in the game world, and what relationships are possible between them? What is excluded from the game's model of reality?
- **What is the player's subject-position?** Individual or collective? Manager or worker? Conqueror or inhabitant? What kinds of agency are available, and what kinds are foreclosed?
- **What is the game's model of conflict?** Is conflict zero-sum or positive-sum? Is it resolved through force, negotiation, or structural transformation? Is it interpersonal, systemic, or both?
- **What is the game's model of time?** Linear-progressive, cyclical, or something else? What counts as "forward" movement?
- **What victory conditions exist?** What does "winning" look like? What does "losing" look like? Is there a stable state that is neither winning nor losing?

#### IV. Ideological Reproduction

- **What worldview does the game naturalize?** What assumptions are embedded so deeply in the mechanics that they are invisible to the player?
- **What does the game exclude?** What aspects of reality are absent from the game's model? What would the game look like if those aspects were included?
- **What "common sense" does the game produce?** After playing, what does the player take for granted about how systems work, what motivates people, and what constitutes rational behavior?

#### V. Contradictions and Possibilities

- **Where does the game contradict itself?** Where do its mechanics undermine its narrative, or its narrative critique its mechanics?
- **Where does the game exceed its ideology?** Are there moments, mechanics, or emergent behaviors that point beyond the game's dominant framework?
- **What would a counter-design look like?** If you were to redesign this game to embody a different set of assumptions, what would change? What would be preserved?

## Conclusion: Games After Capital

This analysis has argued that videogames are not merely shaped by capitalist relations of production but are active participants in the reproduction of capitalist ideology — that their mechanics, systems, and incentive structures train players to inhabit the world as capitalist subjects, to experience accumulation as pleasure, competition as natural, extraction as rational, and domination as victory.

But the analysis must end by insisting on its own incompleteness. If historical materialism teaches anything, it is that no system of domination is total, no hegemony is without cracks, no reproduction of ideology is perfectly efficient. Games contain within themselves the resources for their own critique — not because designers are secretly subversive, but because the interactive form necessarily opens spaces of contingency, interpretation, and resistance that static media do not.

The question is not whether games can be "fixed" — whether we can design our way to liberation through better mechanics. The question is whether game design can contribute to the broader project of imagining and constructing alternatives to the present order. The answer is not guaranteed. The tools are compromised. The institutions are hostile. The market selects for compliance. But the possibility remains, and it remains because the fundamental promise of the interactive form — that the player can act, that the system can be changed, that the outcome is not predetermined — is a promise that exceeds the ideological uses to which it is currently put.

That promise is worth taking seriously. Not because games will save us, but because the capacity to imagine systems that work differently is a precondition for building them. And games, for all their complicity with the world as it is, remain one of the most powerful tools we have for imagining the world as it could be.
