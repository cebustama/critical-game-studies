# The Apparatus and the Atmosphere: Military-Entertainment Complex as Ideological System

## Thread 5 — Military-Entertainment Complex & Propaganda

### Synthesis of Seiwald (2021), Martin (2023), and the Materialist Analysis

---

## I. Theoretical Reconciliation: Gramsci, Althusser, and the Two Faces of Ideological Reproduction

The materialist analysis (Part V) reads games through Gramsci: hegemony operates by making contingent arrangements feel like common sense, and games are powerful hegemonic instruments because they do not merely depict ideological positions but *proceduralize* them. The player who spends hundreds of hours optimizing resource extraction in *Civilization* has not been told that efficient extraction is good — they have *experienced* it as good. This is consent-production through practice.

Seiwald's analysis of Cold War shooters works through Althusser instead. Where Gramsci asks how ruling-class worldviews become common sense, Althusser asks how institutions reproduce the conditions of production by constituting individuals as subjects who recognize themselves within ideology. The Ideological State Apparatus does not persuade — it *interpellates*. It hails the individual, and the individual, by turning around, becomes a subject who has always-already accepted their position within the system. For Seiwald, the military shooter is an ISA: it does not argue that the player should identify with the American soldier; it places the player inside the soldier's body, behind the soldier's rifle, and the identification is accomplished before any argument begins.

These frameworks are complementary, not competing, but they see different things.

**What Gramsci sees that Althusser misses:** Gramsci's hegemony is never total. It must be constantly renegotiated because it operates through consent, and consent can be withdrawn. This creates analytical space for contradiction — for games that simultaneously reproduce and undermine dominant ideology. The deepened analysis (Section II.1) demonstrates this with *Shadow of the Colossus* and *Spec Ops: The Line*, games whose mechanics reward violence while their aesthetic and narrative registers code that violence as loss. Althusser's ISA framework, in its strong form, tends toward structural determinism — the apparatus reproduces ideology, and the subject is constituted by it. There is less room in Althusser for the moment when the apparatus misfires, when interpellation fails, when the subject produced by the system turns that system's logic against itself.

**What Althusser sees that Gramsci misses:** Althusser's framework foregrounds the *institutional* dimension of ideology in ways Gramsci's does not. Gramsci's hegemony is diffuse — it operates through culture, education, media, religion, all of civil society. Althusser's ISA concept specifies the *mechanisms* through which institutions do ideological work: through rules, through practices, through material rituals that constitute subjects. Applied to games, this produces a more precise analytical instrument. It is one thing to say (with Gramsci) that *Call of Duty* naturalizes military intervention as common sense. It is another to say (with Althusser) that the game's specific rule-structures — the respawn mechanic, the killstreak reward, the mission briefing, the first-person perspective — function as material rituals that constitute the player as a specific kind of subject: a subject for whom enemies are targets, death is temporary, and violence is the grammar of agency.

**The synthesis:** Games operate as ideological systems on both registers simultaneously. At the Gramscian level, they produce consent — they make militarized worldviews feel natural, inevitable, even pleasurable. At the Althusserian level, they interpellate — they constitute the player as a subject-position (soldier, commander, operator) through rule-governed practices that do not require conscious agreement. The Gramscian analysis explains *why players accept* the military shooter's worldview; the Althusserian analysis explains *how the game produces* that acceptance before the player has a chance to evaluate it.

Martin's contribution operates at a different level of abstraction. Rather than theorizing the internal mechanics of ideological reproduction, Martin documents the *institutional infrastructure* that connects military organizations to media production — the Pentagon's entertainment liaison offices, the exchange of technical consultation for favorable depiction, the revolving door between military service and media careers. Martin's framework is less theoretically elaborate than either Gramsci or Althusser, but it supplies something both need: the concrete institutional history that connects ideological production to state power. Gramsci's hegemony and Althusser's ISA both risk abstraction without this institutional grounding. Martin shows that the military-entertainment complex is not a metaphor but an organizational reality with specific actors, specific transactions, and specific material interests.

Taken together, the three frameworks produce a layered analysis: Martin identifies the institutional apparatus; Althusser identifies the mechanisms through which that apparatus constitutes subjects; Gramsci identifies the broader hegemonic field within which those subjects give consent to military power as common sense.

## II. The Spectrum of Propaganda: From Overt Apparatus to Atmospheric Hegemony

Seiwald identifies a crucial distinction between overt and covert ideological operation in military games. *America's Army* (2002), produced directly by the U.S. Army as a recruitment tool, is an overt ISA — its institutional origin is transparent, its purpose acknowledged. *Call of Duty*, produced by a commercial studio with no formal military sponsorship, operates covertly — its ideological function is concealed by its commodity form. The player buys entertainment, not propaganda, and the entertainment functions as propaganda precisely because it is not recognized as such.

This distinction is analytically productive but requires expansion into a fuller spectrum. The relationship between military power and game ideology operates through at least four distinct modes, arranged from most to least institutionally visible:

### Mode 1: Direct State Production

*America's Army* is the paradigmatic case. The U.S. Army funded, developed, and distributed the game as an explicit recruitment instrument. The game was free — the commodity form was suspended because the product's value was not commercial but ideological. The game's design choices were directly shaped by military objectives: the player always plays as a U.S. soldier (the opposing team sees themselves as U.S. forces too, a perspectival trick that makes it structurally impossible to play as the enemy); training sequences replicate actual Army training protocols; the game enforces rules of engagement, penalizing the player for shooting civilians or teammates.

As an Althusserian ISA, *America's Army* is almost schematic. The game literally interpellates the player as a soldier-subject through rule-governed practice. The training sequence is not a tutorial in the ludic sense — it is a material ritual that constitutes the player within military discipline. You learn to follow orders, to check your fire, to move in formation. The game does not argue that military service is valuable; it places you inside the practice of military service and lets the practice do the ideological work.

But the overt ISA has a structural limitation: its transparency undermines its effectiveness. Because *America's Army*'s institutional origin is visible, players can maintain critical distance. The game was widely discussed as propaganda, and that discussion partially immunized players against its interpellative force. This is the paradox of the overt apparatus: the more visible its mechanism, the less effective its operation.

### Mode 2: State-Assisted Commercial Production

Martin documents the middle zone: commercial games that receive military consultation, technical access, or logistical support in exchange for favorable depiction. The *Call of Duty* franchise has drawn on military advisors for motion capture, weapons modeling, and tactical authenticity. The relationship is transactional — the military gains cultural legitimacy; the studio gains production value — but it is not directive. The studio retains creative control, and the military's influence operates through access rather than authority.

This mode is more ideologically effective than direct production because the commodity form provides cover. The player purchases *Call of Duty* as entertainment, and the military's involvement in production is invisible during play. The game's ideological operation is concealed by its market positioning — it is a product, not a program, and the consumer's relationship to it is one of choice, not conscription.

### Mode 3: Autonomous Reproduction Without Institutional Contact

Seiwald's most important insight is that the military-entertainment complex does not require direct institutional connections to function. The *Call of Duty* franchise would reproduce military ideology even without military consultants, because the ideological raw material — the narrative templates, the enemy typologies, the moral frameworks — circulates freely in the broader culture. The game's developers do not need the Pentagon to tell them that Russian ultranationalists make compelling villains; the broader discursive field of post-Cold War American culture has already established this as common sense.

This is where the Gramscian and Althusserian frameworks converge most productively. At the Gramscian level, military hegemony has been so thoroughly established that cultural producers reproduce it spontaneously — not because they are coerced or directed but because militarized worldviews have become the invisible architecture of the genre. At the Althusserian level, the genre itself functions as an ISA: the conventions of the military shooter (first-person perspective, killstreak rewards, team-based competitive structure, respawn mechanics) constitute a rule-system that interpellates the player as a military subject regardless of any specific game's narrative content.

This is the mode that requires the most analytical attention, because it is the mode in which ideology is most effectively concealed. When *America's Army* reproduces military ideology, the mechanism is visible. When *Call of Duty: Modern Warfare* reproduces military ideology without direct state involvement, the mechanism has been naturalized — absorbed into the genre conventions that designers and players alike treat as given.

### Mode 4: Structural Militarism Without Military Content

The final mode extends beyond games with explicit military settings. The battle royale genre — *Fortnite*, *PUBG*, *Apex Legends* — strips military ideology of its narrative content while preserving its structural logic. There are no nations, no geopolitical stakes, no identifiable enemies. There is only a shrinking space, scarce resources, and the imperative to eliminate all other players. This is the military logic of territorial control and enemy elimination abstracted to its pure form — warfare without war, militarized space without military justification.

The battle royale's ideological operation is particularly insidious because it appears apolitical. The absence of military narrative content creates the illusion that the game has no military ideology. But the structure — the paradrop insertion, the weapon scavenging, the zone contraction that forces engagement, the last-player-standing win condition — reproduces the spatial logic of military operation. The player learns to read terrain tactically, to calculate risk, to treat other human players as threats to be neutralized. The military subject-position is constructed without the military signifier.

Neither Seiwald nor Martin addresses this mode, and it represents the most significant gap in both analyses.

## III. Mechanics as Apparatus: How Specific Systems Do Ideological Work

Seiwald's four propaganda modes — land seizure, false games and intrigues, lacking morality, and media/mind control — are identified through narrative content analysis. The enemy in Cold War shooters seizes territory, operates through deception, lacks moral restraint, and uses media for manipulation. These are the discursive attributes assigned to the Communist enemy, and Seiwald tracks them effectively through cutscenes, dialogue, and mission framing.

But the analysis remains narratological where it should be mechanical. The deepened analysis (Section II.1) argued that the critical question is not whether a game contains military content but what work the mechanics do within the system. Applying this principle to the military shooter produces a different and more fundamental analysis:

**The first-person perspective** is not a neutral camera position. It is a subject-position that collapses the distance between player and avatar. The player does not observe the soldier; the player *is* the soldier. The gun occupies the lower-right quadrant of the screen at all times — it is the most persistent visual element in the game, more constant than any landscape or character. The world is perceived through, around, and beyond the weapon. This is not a representation of military experience; it is the construction of a perceptual field in which the weapon is the organizing principle of spatial awareness. The player learns, through hours of practice, to perceive the world as a field of potential targets.

**The respawn mechanic** transforms the player's relationship to death. In a military context, death is irreversible — the foundational fact that gives warfare its moral weight. In *Call of Duty* multiplayer, death is a five-second interruption. The player dies, watches a killcam, and reappears. This mechanical fact does not "trivialize" death in a simple sense — it *reorganizes the ontology of violence*. Violence without death is not violence at all; it is a performance, a game, a system of point-scoring. The respawn mechanic allows the player to experience the pleasures of combat (tactical decision-making, spatial awareness, reflexive skill) while externalizing its costs. This is the ideological core: not that war is good, but that its worst consequence has been removed from the simulation.

**The killstreak reward system** (introduced in *Call of Duty 4: Modern Warfare*, 2007) establishes a positive feedback loop between killing and capacity. Kill enough enemies in sequence and the game rewards you with enhanced destructive power — UAV surveillance, airstrike authorization, attack helicopter deployment. This is M-C-M′ transposed into the register of violence: invest kills, generate surplus killing capacity, reinvest. The system teaches that violence *compounds* — that killing efficiently produces the means for killing more efficiently. This is not a representation of military doctrine; it is a procedural argument that violence is a self-amplifying resource, and that the optimal relationship to violence is accumulative.

**The mission briefing structure** interpellates the player within a chain of command. Before each campaign mission, a commanding officer establishes the situation, identifies the objective, and assigns the player's role. The player does not choose the mission; the player receives it. The game constitutes the player as a subject who acts within authorized parameters — whose agency is real but bounded by institutional authority. Disobedience is not mechanically modeled; there is no button for refusing an order. The player can fail to execute the mission (by dying or running out of time), but they cannot *refuse* it. The absence of refusal as a mechanical option is itself the strongest ideological statement the game makes.

**The enemy AI** constitutes the other side of the subject-position. Enemies in military shooters are procedurally generated targets — they spawn, they patrol, they shoot, they die. They have no interiority, no motivations visible to the player, no capacity for surrender or negotiation in most implementations. They are, in Althusserian terms, not subjects at all — they are the constitutive outside against which the player's subjectivity is defined. The soldier-subject requires the target-object. The game produces both simultaneously.

## IV. The Black Ops Contradiction: Where the Apparatus Misfires

Seiwald identifies the most analytically productive case in the corpus: *Call of Duty: Black Ops* (2010) both presents and undermines Cold War ideology. The game's campaign follows CIA operative Alex Mason through Cold War flashpoints — Cuba, Vietnam, Soviet facilities — deploying the standard friend-foe discriminations of the genre. But the narrative reveals that Mason has been brainwashed by the Soviets, that his memories are unreliable, and that the missions he has performed may not have occurred as he remembers them. The player who has spent hours killing enemies in service of American objectives discovers that the subjective ground of those actions was compromised from the beginning.

This is where the Gramscian framework proves indispensable. Althusser's ISA, in its strong form, cannot account for this moment — the apparatus interpellates, the subject is constituted, and the process is complete. But Gramsci's hegemony, because it is always contested and never total, provides analytical space for the game to turn its own ideological operation against itself. The brainwashing narrative does not cancel the hours of interpellative practice the player has undergone — the player has still killed, still followed orders, still inhabited the soldier-subject. But it retrospectively reframes that practice as a performance whose ground was never stable. The player discovers that they were interpellated by a system that was itself a fabrication.

The deepened analysis (Section IV, Layer 4: Contradictions and Excess) argued that games' most productive moments are those where mechanics contradict narrative or where player behavior exceeds designed intention. *Black Ops* produces a specific version of this: the narrative critique is directed at the very subject-position that the mechanics have constructed. The game spends six hours building the military subject through its apparatus — then reveals that the apparatus was, within the fiction, a weapon deployed against the subject.

This does not make *Black Ops* a critical or anti-war game. The multiplayer — where the vast majority of play hours accumulate — reproduces the standard military shooter structure without any narrative complication. The campaign's ideological self-undermining is contained within a single-player mode that most players complete once and never revisit, while the multiplayer's ideological reproduction operates continuously and indefinitely. The contradiction is real but asymmetric: the critical gesture is a bounded event; the hegemonic reproduction is a persistent structure.

The case connects to the deepened analysis's discussion of *Spec Ops: The Line*, which weaponizes the shooter's own mechanics against the player's comfort more thoroughly. But *Black Ops* is the more ideologically interesting case precisely because its self-undermining is *unintentional* in a way *Spec Ops* is not. *Spec Ops* is designed as critique; its developers knew what they were doing. *Black Ops* stumbles into critique through the internal logic of its Cold War espionage narrative — the genre's paranoid structure (trust no one, question everything) turns out to apply to the game itself. The apparatus generates its own contradiction not through authorial intent but through the surplus meanings that narrative complexity produces when it operates within an ideological system.

This is what Gramsci means by the cracks in hegemony: not that dominant ideology fails spectacularly, but that it succeeds imperfectly, leaving residues of meaning that can be activated against the dominant reading by players positioned to notice them.

## V. What the Sources Miss: Multiplayer, Free-to-Play, and the Social Production of Military Subjects

### 5.1 Multiplayer as Social Apparatus

Both Seiwald and Martin analyze military games primarily as texts consumed by individual players. Neither adequately addresses what happens when the military shooter becomes a *social practice* — when players perform military roles not in isolation but in teams, clans, and competitive hierarchies.

The multiplayer military shooter adds a dimension that the single-player campaign lacks: *peer-to-peer interpellation*. In *Call of Duty* multiplayer, the player is constituted as a military subject not only by the game's rule-systems but by other players who enforce norms, evaluate performance, and construct social hierarchies organized around martial competence. The clan structure, the ranked competitive ladder, the voice-chat callouts using military-adjacent terminology ("contact left," "he's one-shot," "pushing objective") — these are social practices that extend the game's ideological apparatus beyond the designed system into player-produced culture.

This is interpellation through horizontal sociality rather than vertical authority. The commanding officer in the campaign briefing is a designed element, easily identified as an ideological construction. The teammate who calls you out for poor tactical positioning is a peer whose authority derives from shared practice. The social enforcement of military norms in multiplayer is more effective than the designed enforcement in single-player because it is produced by the community rather than imposed by the developer.

### 5.2 Free-to-Play Military Games and the Monetization of Militarism

*War Thunder* (2013) and *World of Tanks* (2010) operate in a register that neither Seiwald nor Martin addresses: the free-to-play military simulation whose economic model is inseparable from its ideological function.

In *War Thunder*, the player progresses through "research trees" organized by nation — unlocking increasingly advanced vehicles from World War II through the Cold War to the present day. The research tree is the tech tree of *Civilization* transposed into military-industrial history: progress is measured by the development of increasingly lethal machinery, and the player advances by accumulating enough experience (through combat) to unlock the next tier of destruction. The free-to-play model accelerates this progression for paying players, creating a system in which the literal expenditure of money produces the figurative expenditure of military-industrial development. The player buys progress, and progress is measured in armament.

The national research trees deserve specific attention. The player chooses a nation and commits to its military-industrial trajectory. Playing the Soviet tree means inhabiting the material history of Soviet weapons development — the T-34, the IS-2, the MiG-21. The game does not narrate this history politically; it presents each vehicle as a set of performance characteristics (armor thickness, gun caliber, engine horsepower). The political history that produced these machines — the Eastern Front's twenty-seven million Soviet dead, the Cold War arms race, the proxy wars in which these weapons were deployed — is absent. What remains is the machine, stripped of its history, offered as a consumer commodity. This is a specific ideological operation: the depoliticization of military hardware, its transformation from instrument of state violence into collectible object.

### 5.3 The Noriega Case: Law, Likeness, and the Limits of Appropriation

The *Noriega v. Activision Blizzard* case (2014) occupies a different analytical position. Manuel Noriega, the former Panamanian dictator, sued Activision for using his likeness in *Call of Duty: Black Ops II* (2012) without permission. The case was dismissed on First Amendment grounds — the court ruled that the game's use of Noriega's likeness was protected expression.

The case is significant less for its legal outcome than for what it reveals about the relationship between military history and commercial entertainment. *Black Ops II* incorporated Noriega as a character in a narrative that framed the U.S. invasion of Panama within a broader Cold War storyline. The game presented a real military operation — Operation Just Cause, in which approximately 500 Panamanian civilians were killed — as the backdrop for a fictional espionage thriller. The legal question was whether Activision had the right to appropriate Noriega's likeness; the ideological question is what it means that a real military intervention, with real casualties, can be repackaged as entertainment and sold as a commodity.

The materialist analysis (Part I.4) argued that the live-service model transforms the player from consumer into resource. The Noriega case demonstrates a parallel transformation: historical actors become raw material for commercial entertainment, their political significance extracted and processed into narrative content. Noriega-as-historical-figure and Noriega-as-game-character are related by the same logic that connects coltan-in-the-ground to coltan-in-the-controller — both are instances of extraction, one material, the other semiotic.

## VI. Applying the Framework: What the Military Shooter Teaches

Returning to the five-layer analytical framework established in the deepened analysis:

**Layer 1 — Material Archaeology:** The military shooter is produced by studios employing hundreds of workers under crunch conditions, funded by publishers expecting blockbuster returns, distributed through platforms that extract rent, and consumed on hardware built from extracted minerals. Martin's institutional analysis adds that the military itself participates in this production chain — providing consultation, access, and legitimacy in exchange for favorable cultural representation. The material base is not merely capitalist but *militarized-capitalist*: the state's coercive apparatus and the market's productive apparatus are intertwined at the level of production, not just representation.

**Layer 2 — Systemic Grammar:** The verbs are shoot, move, reload, throw, call in support. The nouns are player, enemy, weapon, objective, killstreak. The syntax is: move through space, identify targets, eliminate targets, capture objectives, accumulate score. The absent verbs — negotiate, refuse, surrender, mourn, retreat — define the system's ideological boundaries as precisely as the present ones.

**Layer 3 — Ideological Operation:** After hundreds of hours of multiplayer, the player has internalized a specific set of assumptions: that space is naturally contested, that strangers are presumptive threats, that problems are solved through superior positioning and accurate fire, that death is temporary and its costs are measured in points rather than grief, that tactical competence is the highest form of agency. These are not beliefs the player has been persuaded to adopt; they are habits the player has been trained to perform.

**Layer 4 — Contradictions and Excess:** The *Black Ops* brainwashing narrative. The emergent anti-war readings of *Spec Ops: The Line*. The way team-based multiplayer produces solidarity and care (players who learn each other's habits, who sacrifice tactical advantage to revive teammates, who build friendships through shared practice) within a system designed around competition and elimination. The contradiction between the game's procedural argument (violence is efficient) and the player's phenomenological experience (the hundredth kill feels nothing like the first — habituation is its own critique).

**Layer 5 — Positionality:** This analysis is produced from within the Western academic tradition that generates both the critical theory used to analyze military games and the cultural context that produces them. The analysis can identify the military shooter's ideological operations but cannot fully account for how these games function in non-Western contexts — how *Call of Duty* is received by players in countries that have been subject to American military intervention, or how *War Thunder*'s national research trees are experienced by players whose nations' military histories are traumatic rather than triumphant. These reception contexts would produce different and potentially more acute analyses than the one offered here.

## VII. What Remains to Be Done

This synthesis establishes the theoretical framework and identifies the key mechanical operations of military-entertainment ideology. Three specific directions remain underexplored:

First, the **Cold War thread** (Thread 6) should build directly on the Althusser-Gramsci synthesis developed here. Seiwald's four propaganda modes need mechanical, not merely narrative, analysis — how do Cold War games construct the Communist enemy through rule-systems rather than cutscenes? The *Command & Conquer: Red Alert* series, which Seiwald notes assigns nuclear weapon use to the Soviets in a reversal of historical fact, deserves sustained mechanical attention.

Second, the **relationship between military shooters and the broader FPS genre** needs examination. The genre conventions that constitute the military shooter's ideological apparatus — first-person perspective, respawn, killstreak rewards — migrated from and to non-military contexts. *Doom* (1993) established the grammar; the military shooter politicized it; the battle royale abstracted it. Tracing these migrations would reveal how military ideology enters game systems that appear to have no military content.

Third, the **phenomenological dimension** flagged in the deepened analysis (Section VIII) is particularly urgent for this thread. The felt experience of pulling a trigger, the proprioceptive knowledge of recoil control, the adrenaline of a close firefight — these embodied experiences are central to the military shooter's ideological effectiveness and cannot be adequately captured by structural analysis alone. The work of Vivian Sobchack on embodied spectatorship and Shoshana Amielle Magnet's work on biometric technologies and bodies may provide frameworks.
