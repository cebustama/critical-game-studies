## Technique 1: Reassigning American Atrocities to Other Nations

The most brazen form of propaganda in military games is taking documented US actions and attributing them to enemy states. The defining case is **Call of Duty: Modern Warfare** (2019, Infinity Ward/Activision). In the game's eleventh mission, set in the fictional country of "Urzikstan," rebel leader Farah tells the player about the "Highway of Death" — a road the Russians bombed during their invasion, killing people trying to escape. The visual design — burnt-out vehicles, grey dust, cracked asphalt — is modeled directly on photographs of the real Highway of Death.

The real Highway of Death is Highway 80 between Kuwait City and the Iraqi border. On February 26–27, 1991, US-led coalition forces attacked a retreating Iraqi military convoy, killing an estimated 500–1,000 people. Journalist Seymour Hersh documented a US tank platoon opening fire on 350 surrendered, unarmed Iraqi soldiers. Evidence emerged that the column included civilians, foreign workers, and refugees. Former US Attorney General Ramsey Clark cited the attack in war crimes allegations. The game takes this specific event — name, visual iconography, and all — and makes Russia responsible.

The technique extends beyond this single mission. Modern Warfare amalgamates multiple real conflicts (Afghanistan, Syria, the Benghazi embassy attack) into "Urzikstan," creating what VICE called an "Ur-Country" — a prototypical "Over There" where Russian forces deploy chemical weapons on a massive scale while the game ignores the US military's own documented use of white phosphorus in Fallujah. Russia banned sales entirely. Sony pulled it from the Russian PlayStation Store.

The **Command & Conquer: Red Alert** series (1996–2008, Westwood Studios/EA) performs a subtler version across the strategy genre. The United States remains the only nation to have used nuclear weapons in war. Yet across three Red Alert games, nuclear aggression consistently originates from the Soviet side. The series systematically reverses the historical direction of nuclear threat.

## Technique 2: Manufacturing Fictional WMDs to Retroactively Justify Real Wars

The Call of Duty: Modern Warfare trilogy functions as a narrative machine that answers a specific question: *What if the War on Terror's justifications had been real?* The United States invaded Iraq in 2003 on the premise that Saddam Hussein possessed weapons of mass destruction. No such weapons existed. The Modern Warfare series solves this political problem by populating its fictional battlefields with chemical weapons, dirty bombs, and nuclear devices that actually work — giving players the experience of a world where WMD threats are genuine and military intervention is unambiguously justified.

**Six Days in Fallujah** (Highwire Games/Victura, early access 2023) performs a related operation through omission. The game depicts the Second Battle of Fallujah (November 2004), one of the bloodiest engagements of the Iraq War. In reality, US forces used white phosphorus against combatants and, evidence suggests, civilians — a chemical weapon that burns flesh to the bone. Victura confirmed the game will not feature white phosphorus as a usable weapon. The game also omits that the US military barricaded men aged 15–55 inside the city despite leaflets telling civilians to evacuate.

## Technique 3: Glorifying Controversial Figures and Whitewashing Their Records

**Call of Duty: Black Ops Cold War** (2020, Treyarch/Activision) features a meticulously rendered CGI Ronald Reagan who authorizes the player's covert missions. His opening speech positions him as a principled commander of unimpeachable integrity. His actual legacy — the Iran-Contra scandal, the AIDS crisis non-response, illegal covert operations in Central America — is either omitted or reframed. The Iran hostage crisis is rewritten as a Soviet-backed plot. The game's "good ending" requires loyal CIA service. The "bad ending" — siding against the CIA — results in nuclear apocalypse, literally equating opposition to American intelligence operations with armageddon.

**Call of Duty: Black Ops II** (2012, Treyarch) went further by hiring Lt. Col. Oliver North — convicted (later reversed on appeal) for his central role in the Iran-Contra affair — as a consultant. North also voiced himself and appeared in promotional videos. North was involved in illegally selling weapons to Iran and funding Nicaraguan rebels accused of human rights abuses. The game's 1980s storyline turns this theater into an exciting spy thriller. Treyarch studio head Mark Lamia defended the hire by calling North "the most well-known covert operations [person]."

## Technique 4: Framing Illegal Operations as Heroic and Necessary

The entire Black Ops sub-franchise is built on reframing illegal CIA operations as dark but necessary heroism. The tagline — "Here's history, ours is the one you don't know about" — positions illegal covert operations as secret heroism. The Bay of Pigs invasion, MACV-SOG operations in Vietnam (illegal cross-border missions), and the CIA's MKUltra mind-control program are all depicted, reframed as chapters in a covert war against existential Soviet threats.

The **Tom Clancy franchise** — Rainbow Six, Ghost Recon, Splinter Cell, The Division — operates similarly across dozens of titles. **Ghost Recon: Wildlands** (2017) depicts Bolivia as a narco-state requiring rescue by American special forces, prompting Bolivia's government to file a formal diplomatic complaint with the French embassy. **Tom Clancy's Elite Squad** (2020 mobile game) depicted protesters seeking an "egalitarian utopia" as secretly controlled by a terrorist organization — released during the Black Lives Matter protests, using a raised-fist symbol associated with the movement.

The Division series casts players as secret Homeland Security "sleeper agents" authorized to use lethal force against American citizens without trial. Each of these franchises normalizes extralegal violence by embedding it in gameplay loops where the player has no option but to participate.

## Technique 5: Constructing Dehumanizing Enemy Stereotypes

Academic research confirms that military games systematically construct dehumanizing enemy archetypes along racial and national lines. A University of Michigan study (Saleem & Anderson) found that playing violent terrorism-themed video games increased anti-Arab attitudes even when Arabs weren't explicitly depicted. Academic Vít Šisler documented how Western games flatten diverse Muslim and Arab identities into social typologies operating within a broader framework of terrorism and hostility. Developer Rami Ismail stated: "Muslim blood is the cheapest on Earth right now. American blood is the most expensive. That's all you are doing in Call of Duty."

**Command & Conquer: Generals** (2003) featured an Arab faction called the "Global Liberation Army" equipped with car bombs, suicide bombers, and AK-47s — contrasted with the advanced technology of US and Chinese factions.

**Homefront** (2011), with a story attributed to Red Dawn director John Milius, originally cast China as the invading enemy before switching to North Korea to avoid alienating the Chinese market. The Nichi Bei Weekly criticized the game's depiction of Korean forces running death camps on American soil.

## Technique 6: Normalization Through Game Mechanics

Strategy and simulation games encode imperial and military logic into their fundamental design. The **Civilization** series' 4X framework — "eXplore, eXpand, eXploit, eXterminate" — inherently reinforces colonial and imperial structures. Postcolonial analysis using Gayatri Spivak's framework demonstrates that there is no path to victory that does not employ the logics of empire.

**Hearts of Iron IV** (Paradox Interactive) presents propaganda through systematic omission. The game deliberately excludes the Holocaust, concentration camps, and systematic purges. Academic analysis concluded this perpetuates revisionist myths such as the "clean Wehrmacht."

Military simulation games blur the line between entertainment and training. The **Arma** series shares its technological DNA with **Virtual Battlespace (VBS)**, a military training platform used by over 50 militaries worldwide. Bohemia Interactive was saved from bankruptcy when the US Army began using a mod of Operation Flashpoint for soldier training. The company's simulation division was later acquired by defense contractor BAE Systems for $200 million.

Ian Bogost's concept of "procedural rhetoric" explains the mechanism: games persuade not through narrative alone but through the rules players must follow. The player doesn't just watch a soldier follow orders — they enact the logic of the chain of command through gameplay itself.

## Technique 7: The "Apolitical" Defense

Nearly every controversial military game deploys the same rhetorical shield: *this is not political*. The pattern is so consistent it constitutes its own propaganda technique.

- **Call of Duty: Modern Warfare** (2019) — Infinity Ward art director Joel Emslie: "No. We're just making a game."
- **Six Days in Fallujah** — Victura CEO Peter Tamte initially: "Not trying to make a political commentary."
- **Black Ops II** — Treyarch studio head Mark Lamia: "We're not trying to make a political statement with our game."
- **Ghost Recon: Wildlands** — Ubisoft: "A work of fiction... for entertainment purposes."
- **The Division 2** — Creative director Terry Spier: "We're definitely not making any political statements" — about a game featuring civil war at the White House.
- **Ubisoft corporate** — COO Alf Condelius: "We cannot be openly political in our games" because it's "bad for business."

The "apolitical" defense serves a dual function: it shields studios from controversy while naturalizing the political assumptions embedded in their games. When war against Arabs, Russians, or Latin Americans is presented as the default setting of entertainment rather than a political statement, the ideology becomes invisible — which is precisely how effective propaganda works.

## The Institutional Infrastructure

The propaganda techniques documented above do not emerge from individual creative decisions alone. They are supported by a documented institutional infrastructure.

### Direct State Production

The US Army developed and published **America's Army** (2002–2022), conceived by Colonel Casey Wardynski after the Army missed its recruiting targets. Developed at the Naval Postgraduate School's MOVES Institute, the game cost approximately $50 million, attracted over 10 million registered users, and was by the Army's own assessment its "most cost-effective" recruitment tool. A 2008 MIT study found that nearly 30% of Americans aged 16–24 expressed a more positive impression of the Army because of the game.

The Army also established the **Institute for Creative Technologies (ICT)** at USC in 1999, funded with an initial $45 million contract that has grown to cumulative investment likely exceeding $500 million. ICT produced **Full Spectrum Warrior** (2004), which began as a pure Army R&D project before being released commercially — the Xbox version contained a hidden cheat code that unlocked the actual Army training version.

### Embedded Military Consultants

Retired Lt. Col. Hank Keirsey served as military advisor on the Call of Duty franchise from 2004 through Black Ops II. Captain Dale Dye (USMC, ret.) consulted on the original Medal of Honor franchise. Oliver North served as script consultant, voice actor, and promotional figure for Black Ops II.

### The Revolving Door

Dave Anthony, writer and director of Black Ops and Black Ops II (combined revenue exceeding $3 billion), was hired in 2014 as a Nonresident Senior Fellow at the Atlantic Council's Scowcroft Center for Strategy and Security. He was personally recruited by Steven Grundman, a former Pentagon official impressed by a Call of Duty storyline. Six Days in Fallujah CEO Peter Tamte previously founded Destineer, a company that produced training simulations for the US military.

### Esports Recruitment

The US Army Esports team was founded in November 2018 after the Army missed its recruiting goal for the first time in 13 years. The team ran fake Xbox controller giveaways on Twitch that redirected to Army recruitment forms. When viewers posted about US war crimes in chat, moderators banned over 300 accounts. Rep. Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez filed an amendment to prohibit military recruitment via gaming platforms; it was defeated 292–126. Assistant Secretary Casey Wardynski — the same officer who created America's Army — stated: "Will we wait until they're 17, or will we start talking to them at age 12, 13, 14, 15?"

### Defense Contractor Integration

BAE Systems acquired Bohemia Interactive Simulations for $200 million. Raytheon Intelligence & Space is a founding sponsor of the US Cyber Games. The Pentagon spent over $150 million on military-themed games or simulations in 1999 alone. By 2015, Pentagon Instruction 5410.16 formally included "electronic games" in the list of media that may benefit military recruiting.

## Games That Resist the Machinery

**Spec Ops: The Line** (2012, Yager Development) is a military shooter designed to destroy the military shooter from within. Its white phosphorus scene forces the player to deploy the chemical weapon, then walk through the aftermath to discover they have massacred civilians. Loading screens shift from gameplay tips to accusations: "Do you feel like a hero yet?" The game was a commercial failure but became a cult classic.

**This War of Mine** (2014, 11 Bit Studios) inverts the power fantasy. Inspired by the Siege of Sarajevo, it puts players in control of civilians scavenging for survival. Characters become sick, depressed, or suicidal based on player decisions. The Polish government added it to the national school curriculum in 2020 — the first video game to receive such recognition.

**Papers, Please** (2013, Lucas Pope) demonstrates how bureaucratic systems transform individuals into instruments of state violence. **Metal Gear Solid** (Hideo Kojima, 1987–2015) represents the longest-running anti-war critique in mainstream gaming.

These games share a structural limitation: they are commercially marginal compared to the franchises they critique. Call of Duty has generated over $30 billion in lifetime revenue. The structural incentives of the industry favor propaganda over its subversion.

## How the System Operates: Five Interlocking Mechanisms

1. **Direct funding creates foundational technology.** The Pentagon funded the computers on which the first video games were built. It funded ICT at USC, the MOVES Institute, and VBS military simulators. This creates a base layer of military-origin technology and design philosophy.

2. **Embedded consultants shape authenticity norms.** Military advisors define what "realistic" means. Their involvement is voluntary, but the effect is to ensure that the military's self-image becomes the default representation.

3. **The "apolitical" defense naturalizes ideology.** By refusing to acknowledge political content, studios transform ideological assumptions into the invisible background of entertainment.

4. **The revolving door integrates gaming into the national security establishment.** Dave Anthony moves from Call of Duty to the Atlantic Council. Peter Tamte moves from military simulation contracts to Six Days in Fallujah. These aren't coincidences — they are career paths.

5. **Commercial incentives align with propaganda.** Military games sell because they offer power fantasies, and power fantasies require enemies who deserve to be killed and wars that deserve to be fought. Studios don't need Pentagon orders to reproduce military ideology — the market rewards it.
